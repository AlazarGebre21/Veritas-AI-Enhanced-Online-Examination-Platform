Abstract
The rapid expansion of digital education and remote recruitment has increased the demand for reliable, scalable, and secure online assessment systems. Traditional examination methods often face limitations such as lack of flexibility, manual administration overhead, scalability challenges, and difficulties in ensuring fairness and candidate identity verification. This report addresses these challenges by designing and documenting a comprehensive enterprise-level online assessment management system that supports multiple stakeholders, including system administrators, enterprise administrators, staff members, candidates, and external payment services.

The proposed system provides a structured, role-based platform to manage the complete lifecycle of digital assessments. Key functionalities include enterprise registration and subscription management, staff and candidate management, exam creation and scheduling, candidate assignment, secure exam delivery, identity verification, automated grading, reporting, and certificate issuance. The system integrates dashboard-based analytics to support informed decision-making at both system-wide and enterprise-specific levels. A modular, use-case-driven design approach clearly defines system behavior, interactions, and responsibilities across different actors.

This project presents \textit{Veritas}, an AI-enhanced online assessment and monitoring system tailored for Ethiopian enterprises and institutions. In alignment with the Digital Ethiopia 2030 Strategy, Veritas emphasizes local data sovereignty through a multi-tenant architecture, enabling independent exam environments for multiple organizations. Key features include secure authentication via enterprise IDs or tokens, continuous identity verification using face recognition, intelligent proctoring (e.g., tab-switch detection, mouse tracking, IP monitoring,...), automated grading for both objective and subjective questions using NLP models, customizable dashboards for exam management and analytics, automated certificate issuance, and simulated subscription modules supporting local payments.

Developed using an Agile Scrum methodology, the prototype employs a microservices architecture (Go for performance-critical components, Python for AI modules), and has been tested for usability, security, and reliability. Evaluation demonstrates improved efficiency, reduced cheating, and enhanced trust, with AI-assisted grading minimizing human bias and bottlenecks. This work contributes to software engineering by providing a blueprint for contextually adaptable, secure assessment systems that foster technological independence in resource-constrained settings, while supporting e-learning, recruitment, and certification processes.

Chapter 1: Introduction
The rapid advancement of digital technologies has fundamentally transformed the landscape of educational and professional evaluations. Globally, the transition from traditional paper-based examinations to digital assessment platforms has been accelerated by the need for greater efficiency, scalability, and immediate feedback. This shift toward digital transformation has allowed academic institutions and enterprise sectors to adopt remote evaluation technologies that reduce logistical overhead, automate grading, and offer better reach across geographical boundaries.
In the Ethiopian context, the adoption of online examination platforms is seeing significant growth, particularly within education and organizational recruitment sectors . As institutions strive to modernize their administrative workflows, there is an increasing reliance on digital tools to manage large-scale assessments. However, many current implementations are either generic platforms or manual processes that are not fully aligned with local infrastructural realities, such as varying internet stability and specific institutional requirements.
The integrity of these digital assessments remains a cornerstone of their success. While the convenience of remote testing is undeniable, ensuring that a candidate’s performance truly reflects their genuine ability is a persistent challenge for educators and HR professionals alike. As the demand for objective human capital evaluation grows, there is a parallel need for systems that can provide a high degree of trust without being prohibitively expensive or technologically inaccessible in resource-constrained environments.
The development of the Veritas system represents a response to this evolving landscape. By leveraging modern web technologies and artificial intelligence, the project aims to bridge the gap between global assessment standards and local institutional needs. This study explores the potential for a locally developed, secure, and intelligent platform that prioritizes data sovereignty and operational efficiency, ultimately contributing to the broader goal of technological independence and digital innovation within Ethiopia.

1.1. Statement of the Problem
In Ethiopia, enterprises and organizations increasingly rely on online assessments for recruitment and academic evaluations. However, there is a significant lack of comprehensive, commercially available platforms tailored to the local digital ecosystem. Currently, organizations often depend on foreign-hosted solutions which present challenges regarding data sovereignty, high subscription costs in foreign currency, and a lack of integration with local identity infrastructures. According to the Ministry of Innovation and Technology (2025), a key objective of the Digital Ethiopia 2030 Strategy is to reduce this dependency on external digital platforms and establish "Digital Sovereignty" by fostering locally developed systems that keep Ethiopian data within national borders.
Existing digital tools are largely restricted to automating objective assessments. While these systems handle simple data well, they are insufficient for managing subjective evaluations at scale. The grading of open-ended essays and descriptive answers remains a manual, labor-intensive process. This reliance on manual grading for subjective content leads to significant bottlenecks, introduces human bias, and results in substantial delays. Current systems fail to provide an automated framework that can accurately interpret and grade complex, high-volume subjective submissions, forcing organizations to choose between the simplicity of objective tests or the inefficiency of manual review.
While global platforms such as TestGorilla, HackerRank, and ProctorU offer sophisticated features like continuous authentication and behavior analytics, they remain largely inaccessible to most Ethiopian organizations. The primary barrier is the high cost of subscription fees required in foreign currency, which is strictly regulated and in short supply within the national economy. Furthermore, these platforms are optimized for high-bandwidth, stable international internet nodes. In the local context, high latency and connectivity fluctuations often result in session timeouts and data loss when interacting with foreign-hosted servers, thereby compromising the reliability of  assessments.
As a result, maintaining examination credibility, preventing cheating, verifying identity, and handling large-scale automated assessments remain significant challenges for Ethiopian institutions and organizations. There is, therefore, a clear need for a secure, and inte-lligent online assessment and examination platform that provides flexibility, integrity, and scalability tailored to the needs of Ethiopian organizations and institutions .

1.3 Scope and Limitations
1.3.1 Scope
This project focuses on the design, development, and evaluation of a prototype AI-enhanced online assessment and monitoring system intended for enterprise and organizational use within the Ethiopian context. The scope of the project is limited to demonstrating the core concepts, architecture, and key functionalities of a secure and intelligent online examination platform.
Within this scope, the project includes:
Design of a multi-tenant system architecture that allows multiple enterprises to manage independent examination environments.
Development of core examination management features, including exam creation, scheduling, and candidate participation.
Implementation of basic authentication mechanisms, such as enterprise-issued credentials or unique examination tokens.
Integration of AI-assisted smart proctoring features at a prototype level, including tab-switch detection, mouse activity tracking, and periodic image capture for identity verification.
Implementation of automated grading for objective-type and essay based questions and basic analytics for performance evaluation and cheating indication.
Development of role-based administrative dashboards for exam management and result visualization.
Simulation of subscription-based access control models.
The project scope excludes full-scale production deployment, large-scale real-world usage, legal compliance certification, and continuous system maintenance. Payment processing and cloud infrastructure are implemented at a demonstration or simulated level only.
The system is tested and demonstrated in a controlled environment to evaluate usability, system performance, and the effectiveness of the AI-based monitoring mechanisms.
1.3.2 Limitations
Despite its comprehensive functionality, the project has certain technical and contextual limitations due to time, resource, and scope constraints:
Platform Restriction: The current phase of the project focuses solely on the web application; no dedicated mobile or desktop application  version is included.
Biometric Authentication: Advanced biometric features such as, fingerprint, or voice verification are excluded due to hardware and privacy constraints.
Human Proctoring: The system relies solely on automated proctoring mechanisms; real-time human invigilation via webcam or video streaming is not integrated.
Dataset and AI Model Limitations: The AI-based behavior analysis relies on lightweight, pre-trained models. As a result, its accuracy may vary depending on lighting, device camera quality, and candidate behavior diversity.
Scalability Constraints: Large-scale, nationwide deployment and integration with government-level exam systems are beyond the current project phase but may be considered for future iterations.

1.4 Methodology
The methodology for this project is anchored in the Agile framework, specifically utilizing the Scrum methodology, to facilitate an iterative and adaptive development environment. This approach has been selected as the ideal choice over traditional linear models because the development of an AI-driven, security-critical system requires continuous refinement of complex proctoring and semantic analysis layers. By working in iterative cycles, the project can rapidly incorporate stakeholder feedback and adapt to technical challenges inherent in the Ethiopian infrastructure, ensuring that the final product is both technologically robust and contextually relevant.
This methodology prioritizes a blend of comprehensive upfront research and an iterative development cycle encompassing design, implementation, testing, and deployment. The Agile approach specifically enables us to mitigate risks early, such as high-latency network bottlenecks or AI "false positives," by validating functional increments at the end of every sprint.
Our methodology incorporates user-centered design principles, ensuring that stakeholder feedback is integrated during both the initial requirements gathering and the final evaluation phases. This ensures the system remains adaptable to local needs, such as strict compliance with the Ethiopian Data Protection Proclamation and maintaining stability during high-concurrency exams. By following this structured yet flexible path, the project ensures traceability, risk mitigation, and the high-quality delivery of academic objectives.
The methodology comprises two primary components: Research and Requirements Engineering and the System Development Process, detailed in the following subsections.
1.4.1 Research and Requirements Engineering
The foundation of the smart proctoring system is grounded in a systematic and multi-method research process aimed at deeply understanding the security, operational, and usability requirements of target users in Ethiopia. Quantitative and qualitative techniques—including surveys, structured interviews, and comparative analysis of existing platforms—will be utilized to gather empirical and contextual insights from stakeholders involved in high-stakes online examinations.
A. Interviews with HRs and Educators 
This qualitative phase focuses on understanding the high-level administrative and pedagogical needs of those overseeing assessments. Interviews allow for deep-dive discussions into regulatory compliance and the "human" bottlenecks of grading that surveys might miss.
Guiding Questions:
What is the current average turnaround time for grading subjective/essay-based assessments, and how does this impact your recruitment/academic cycles?
What specific behaviors do you currently define as "cheating" that are most difficult to detect in a remote setting?
How critical is data sovereignty (storing data within Ethiopia) to your institution's legal and ethical standards?
What are the most common technical complaints you receive from candidates during online exams?
B. Literature Review
A systematic review of academic papers, technical documentation, and industry reports on virtualization, AI monitoring, and NLP. To avoid "reinventing the wheel," this section ensures the project builds upon existing scientific benchmarks for AI accuracy and container security.
Guiding Questions:
What are the state-of-the-art benchmarks for Transformer-based models in automated subjective grading?
How do current researchers address the trade-off between strict proctoring and user privacy?
What are the known vulnerabilities of process-level isolation (Namespaces/Cgroups) in exam-environment sandboxing?
What are the best practices for handling high-latency connections in real-time WebSocket communication?
C. Observational Studies of Existing Tools A hands-on comparative analysis of global platforms (ProctorU, TestGorilla) and available local systems. Direct observation reveals the "friction points" in user experience and technical barriers (like Forex payment gates) that international tools impose on local users.
Guiding Questions:
At what point in the user journey does the system fail when simulated on a typical 3G/4G Ethiopian network?
How many steps does it take for an administrator to set up a new exam, and can this be simplified for the local context?
How does the system respond to common local hardware constraints (e.g., low-resolution webcams or limited CPU resources)?
What specific proctoring flags are most prone to "false positives" in a typical local testing environment (e.g., background noise or lighting)?
A detailed comparative analysis will be conducted on international solutions (e.g., ProctorU, Respondus) and local Ethiopian platforms. This will identify key gaps, such as high costs and lack of localized support in global tools, limited scalability and AI capabilities in local systems, and frequent user concerns regarding intrusiveness and false-positive rates.
1.4.2 System Development Process
The project follows the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) integrated into an Agile framework, allowing for iterative development of AI components.
Phase 1: Planning and Requirement Analysis
The objective is to define the project scope and technical feasibility.
Activities: Finalizing the Product Backlog and prioritizing features for the Minimum Viable Product (MVP).
Tools: GitHub Projects for task tracking and Trello for high-level milestone management.
Phase 2: System Design and Architecture
Requirements are translated into a technical blueprint.
Architecture: An Event-Driven Microservices Architecture (Choreography-based) is adopted over a monolith. This specific pattern ensures that high-volume proctoring events are handled asynchronously through a message broker, ensuring that a failure in the proctoring log service does not crash the core examination engine or block the candidate's submission path.
Modeling Tools: Visual Paradigm or LucidChart for UML diagrams (Use Case, Sequence, and ER Diagrams).
UI/UX Tools: Figma is used to create interactive high-fidelity prototypes. This ensures stakeholder feedback is gathered before expensive backend development begins.
Phase 3: Technical Implementation (Build)
The actual coding and model training occur in this phase.
Backend Strategy: Leveraging the microservices architecture, we adopt a dual-stack approach. Go (Golang) is utilized for high-performance, concurrency-critical services such as the Real-time Proctoring Event Streamer and the API Gateway. Meanwhile, FastAPI (Python) is used for the AI-intensive services (Subjective Grading and Anomaly Detection). This hybrid approach ensures we benefit from Go's superior throughput for network-heavy tasks and Python’s extensive ecosystem for machine learning.
Continuous Authentication Engine: This is a core security feature implemented within the Python microservice. It utilizes Facial Recognition (OpenCV/DeepFace) and Liveness Detection to verify the candidate's identity at random intervals throughout the exam. This prevents "proxy testing" or student replacement after the initial login.
Frontend: React is chosen over Next.js. Since Veritas is a dashboard-heavy application where SEO is irrelevant, a React SPA provides total control over client-side state, critical for real-time video handling.
Database: PostgreSQL is utilized for its strict ACID compliance, ensuring academic records and grades are never lost or corrupted.
AI & Communication: Transformers (BERT/RoBERTa) for semantic grading and WebSockets for low-latency proctoring logs.
Phase 4: Quality Assurance and Testing
Unit Testing: PyTest (Python services), Go Test (Go services), and Jest (Frontend) for isolated module validation.
Scalability Testing: Apache JMeter to simulate 500+ concurrent users under typical Ethiopian network conditions.
Security Testing: A "Red Team" approach to attempt bypassing proctoring (e.g., tab-switching), targeting a detection rate of >95%.
Phase 5: Documentation
Comprehensive documentation is developed concurrently with the implementation to ensure transparency and long-term maintainability.
Technical Documentation: Swagger/OpenAPI (built-in with FastAPI) for backend API documentation and JSDoc for the React frontend logic.
System Manuals: User guides for examiners (admin portal) and candidates (exam portal), focusing on troubleshooting common local network issues.

Chapter Two: Literature Review
Overview
This chapter presents a critical review of the literature concerning online assessment systems, with a particular focus on their evolution during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. The section establishes the theoretical and empirical foundation for the Veritas Online Assessment system by exploring trends, challenges, and innovations in online proctoring, integrity assurance, and AI-driven automated essay scoring (AES), automated short answer grading(ASAG) and feedback generation, drawing from recent advancements in natural language processing (NLP) and large language models (LLMs).
2.1 Study Related Works
Topuz et al. conducted a systematic review of 22 online assessment systems to identify the emerging trends in online measurement and evaluation during the Emergency Remote Teaching (ERT) period. Their findings emphasized three major features: multi-platform compatibility, enhanced security through biometric authentication, and automated proctoring. Systems like Examity, ProctorU, and Safe Exam Browser were noted for multimodal authentication, integrating webcam, keystroke, and facial recognition.
Complementing this, Kurniati et al. investigated challenges faced by Indonesian teachers across educational levels, highlighting infrastructure limitations, academic dishonesty, and inconsistent student discipline. Teachers employed adaptive strategies, such as shifting from objective to subjective assessments and providing localized solutions like home visits or mobile phone sharing.
Several studies have since expanded on these foundations. Hussein et al.  evaluated commercial proctoring systems, identifying cost, accessibility, and ethical concerns as key barriers. Arnò et al. performed a detailed feature comparison of 29 systems, concluding that a hybrid model combining automated facial detection and human oversight achieved optimal balance between security and user experience. Jia and He proposed an intelligent online proctoring system (IOPS) utilizing AI for facial, eye, and voice monitoring, which successfully reduced cheating incidents in real university exams.
Furthermore, Rahim outlined guidelines for conducting online assessments during emergency remote teaching, stressing the alignment of assessments with learning objectives and the importance of formative evaluation. More recent research by Ali et al. analyzed phased implementations of remote online assessments in medical education, revealing that mock exams and small-group training improved student satisfaction despite technological challenges.
Building on these advancements in proctoring and assessment integrity, recent literature has explored Automated Essay Scoring (AES) and Automated Short Answer Grading (ASAG) using large language models (LLMs) to enhance evaluation efficiency for subjective questions, addressing ERT(Emergency Remote Teaching) gaps in manual grading scalability. Mizumoto and Eguchi (2023) demonstrated GPT-3's effectiveness for AES on TOEFL essays, achieving high human agreement (QWK ~0.7) through zero-shot prompting, enabling instant feedback on linguistic and content traits. Schneider et al. (2024) applied GPT-4 for ASAG, showing reliable scoring of short textual responses in educational benchmarks, particularly for rubric-based exam questions like listing key concepts.
Mansour et al. (2024) evaluated LLMs like GPT-3.5 and Llama for proficiency essay scoring, emphasizing trait-based feedback (e.g., coherence, relevance) with variable but promising correlations to human raters. Kundu and Barbosa (2024) analyzed ChatGPT and Llama on AES datasets, noting LLMs' utility as assistants despite harsher scoring tendencies on nuanced responses. Xiao et al. (2024) proposed a human-AI collaborative framework for AES, improving efficiency by 20% through dual-process workflows that integrate LLM suggestions with educator oversight.

2.2 Identifying Milestones of Related Literature and Finding the Gaps
2.2.1 Milestones
Phase 1: Manual Online Assessments (Pre-2020)
Early online assessment systems focused on basic digital delivery and security. Foster and Layman (2013) compared eight online proctoring systems, analyzing features such as authentication methods, webcam monitoring, lockdown tools, and security risks. They showed that webcam-based monitoring was an important deterrent to cheating. Karim and Shukur (2016) expanded this work by studying authentication methods and proposing a preference-based model, grouping techniques into knowledge-based, possession-based, biometric, and contextual methods.
Strengths: Provided clear frameworks for understanding early systems.Weaknesses: Mostly descriptive, limited real-world testing, and little focus on scalability or low-bandwidth environments.
Phase 2: Emergency Remote Teaching (2020–2021)
COVID-19 forced rapid adoption of online exams, revealing major integrity challenges. Topuz et al. (2022) reviewed systems studied during this period, identifying common features such as desktop-focused platforms, pre-exam face recognition, semi-automatic monitoring, and LMS integration (e.g., Moodle). Other studies compared commercial tools in terms of scalability, authentication, and monitoring.
Strengths: Timely and comprehensive overview of pandemic-driven solutions.Weaknesses: Relied on self-reported data and largely ignored infrastructure limitations in developing regions.
Phase 3: AI and Automation Integration (2021–2024)
Machine learning enabled automated cheating detection. Jia and He (2021/2022) developed an AI-based proctoring system for real-time monitoring, while Tiong and Lee (2021) used deep learning to detect abnormal behavior and network patterns. Review studies reported improved accuracy but also highlighted false positives.
Strengths: Shift toward proactive, automated integrity enforcement.Weaknesses: Dependence on good data and stable internet; limited attention to bias and privacy.
Phase 4: User-Centric Design and Ethics (2024–2025)
Recent studies focus on ethics, privacy, and user experience. Research discusses reduced video surveillance, GDPR compliance, hybrid assessment models, and the need for human oversight in AI systems. Equity and bias across diverse populations are increasingly emphasized.
Strengths: Stronger focus on ethical and human-centered design.Weaknesses: Still early-stage, with limited empirical evidence and Western-centric perspectives.
Phase 5: LLM-Driven Automated Scoring for Essays and Short Answers (2023–2026)
This phase focuses on integrating large language models (LLMs) like GPT-3, GPT-4, ChatGPT, and Llama for Automated Essay Scoring (AES) and Automated Short Answer Grading (ASAG), emphasizing rubric-based evaluation, feedback generation, and human-AI collaboration. Key milestones include Mizumoto and Eguchi (2023), who demonstrated GPT-3's potential for AES on TOEFL essays with high human agreement (QWK ~0.7) via zero-shot prompting; Schneider et al. (2024), who applied GPT-4 for ASAG, achieving reliable scoring on short textual responses; Mansour et al. (2024), evaluating LLMs for proficiency essay scoring with trait-based feedback; Kundu and Barbosa (2024), finding LLMs harsher and less correlated with humans in AES but useful as assistants; and Xiao et al. (2024), proposing a dual-process human-AI framework for AES that improves efficiency and reduces misgrading through collaborative workflows.
Strengths: This phase advances scalability and personalization in online assessments by leveraging LLMs for consistent, instant scoring and detailed feedback, enabling efficient handling of large-scale subjective responses (e.g., essays and short answers) with high agreement to human raters (QWK 0.68–0.88 across studies); it promotes accessibility through open-source models and prompting techniques, reducing educator workload while supporting diverse formats like rubric-based exam questions.
Weaknesses: Despite progress, LLM performance is sensitive to prompt design and training biases, leading to inconsistencies in domain-specific or non-English contexts; studies show variable human-LLM correlations and potential over-harshness/leniency, with limited empirical validation in real-world, resource-constrained environments, raising concerns about reliability without human oversight.
2.2.2 Gaps Identified
Despite advances, significant gaps persist:
Poor Accessibility for Students with Disabilities: A significant but often overlooked limitation in online assessment systems is their lack of accessibility for students with disabilities. Topuz et al. identified that most existing platforms employ restrictive security measures—such as forced browser lockdowns, hidden taskbars, and required software installations—that unintentionally exclude users who depend on assistive technologies like screen readers or adaptive input devices. These constraints prevent such students from navigating exams independently and raise ethical concerns about inclusivity.Hussein et al. also observed that advanced proctoring systems rarely incorporate Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles, focusing instead on surveillance and integrity rather than equitable usability. Similarly, Rahim highlighted that during emergency remote teaching, accessibility and fairness were underemphasized, especially for learners with sensory or cognitive disabilities. Uça Güneş et al.  further confirmed that accessibility policies were inconsistent across institutions, and few systems offered accommodations such as extended time or alternative input modes. Collectively, these studies demonstrate a persistent design bias—prioritizing security and standardization over inclusivity.
The “Security vs. Connectivity” Gap: A persistent limitation in online assessment research lies in the trade-off between exam security and network reliability. Most secure proctoring platforms depend on continuous high-speed internet and uninterrupted video streaming to verify student behavior. However, studies in developing regions reveal that weak digital infrastructure severely impedes assessment validity. Kurniati et al. found that Indonesian teachers frequently abandoned video-based testing due to poor bandwidth and device incompatibility. Similarly, Ali et al.  reported that 57 % of medical students in Pakistan experienced internet instability during live-proctored exams, resulting in high anxiety and incomplete submissions. Hussein et al.  further noted that commercial proctoring tools such as ProctorU and Respondus required intensive data streaming and high computing power, creating inequity between students with and without robust connections. Topuz et al. confirmed that even secure systems like Safe Exam Browser remain dependent on real-time connectivity. Hence, despite advances in digital invigilation, current systems lack adaptive, low-bandwidth monitoring mechanisms capable of maintaining integrity under unstable network conditions. Addressing this limitation is crucial for equitable implementation of online assessments in bandwidth-constrained environments such as Ethiopia.
The “Intelligent Proctoring” Gap:  Another major research gap concerns the absence of mid-level intelligent proctoring systems. Existing literature illustrates a polarization between simple unproctored tools and costly AI-driven systems that require human oversight or aggressive live video analysis. Arnò et al. demonstrated that only high-end commercial platforms provide comprehensive automation, yet these remain financially inaccessible to smaller universities. Jia and He introduced an AI-based system integrating facial and voice analysis, but acknowledged privacy and scalability challenges stemming from continuous data transmission. Tiong and Lee proposed a deep-learning model for cheating detection, though it required GPU-intensive computation and complex setup. Rahim observed that many institutions reverted to Google Forms and LMS quizzes precisely because proctoring tools were technologically demanding.These findings underline the lack of accessible “passive-AI” solutions—systems that ensure exam integrity through lightweight features such as tab-switch detection, periodic image capture, or local face verification without live video streaming. Developing such balanced systems would democratize secure assessments for low-resource educational institutions.
The “Local Customization” Gap: reviewed studies also reveal a significant contextual and regulatory gap. Current online assessment technologies are predominantly developed for Western institutions and seldom consider local privacy laws, language interfaces, or administrative workflows relevant to African higher-education systems. Topuz et al. observed that nearly all systems in their global review were hosted abroad, while Kurniati et al. highlighted the difficulties of adapting foreign platforms to local socio-technical realities. Uça Güneş et al. emphasized the absence of consistent institutional policies within emerging economies, resulting in reliance on externally governed software. Likewise, Tabassum and Ali et al. stressed that assessments remain culturally and infrastructurally mismatched to learners’ environments.Consequently, there is little evidence of indigenous, region-specific assessment frameworks tailored to comply with national data-protection regulations—such as Ethiopia’s draft Personal Data Protection Proclamation—or aligned with local HR and university workflows. This presents an opportunity for Veritas Online Assessment to pioneer a locally hosted, policy-compliant, and culturally responsive assessment ecosystem.
ASAG(Automatic Short Answer Grading) Gaps: Research on short answers remains narrow in application, as Schneider et al. (2024) identifies variability in GPT-4 performance, leading to inconsistent scoring for domain-specific, technical responses. There is a limitation in multilingual and non-english contexts.
AES(Automatic Essay Scoring) Gaps: Human-LLM agreement is variable and often suboptimal, with Kundu and Barbosa (2024) highlighting LLMs' tendency to be harsher on nuanced essays due to biases in training data; Mansour et al. (2024) and Mizumoto and Eguchi (2023) focus primarily on proficiency or linguistic traits, neglecting adaptation to non-English, technical domains and culturally diverse datasets, resulting in potential inequities for global users.
2.3 Lessons Learned from Literature
Three core insights arise:
Infrastructure and Inclusivity Matter: Kurniati et al. and Ali et al. underscored the digital divide’s role in exam integrity, emphasizing flexible system design adaptable to varying connectivity conditions.
Future Assessments Must Be Ethical and Data-Conscious: Works from 2024–2025, stress ethical implementation, transparent data handling, and user-centric approaches.
Integrity Cannot Rely on Trust Alone: Research consistently shows that without technical methods, academic dishonesty is inevitable in remote settings. Teachers attempting to mitigate cheating by simply "changing question types" or "randomizing questions" found these methods insufficient without supervision. So the system must proactively detect behaviors like tab-switching and multiple face detections.
Infrastructure is the Primary Constraint: The most frequent complaint in the literature regarding online exams is technical failure (internet failure, power outages).So The Veritas architecture must be lightweight. Using client-side AI (processing behavior on the user's device rather than streaming heavy video to a server) is a crucial strategy to ensure the system works even with unstable connections.
User Experience Impacts Performance: Complex interfaces increase student anxiety and technical error rates. The literature suggests that students are often unfamiliar with advanced online tools. So The candidate portal must be intuitive and require minimal setup, ensuring that the cognitive load is placed on the exam content, not the software interface.
Consistency: LLMs provide reliable and generalizable scoring across large datasets, outperforming traditional methods in efficiency for AES and ASAG.
Human-AI Collaboration: Frameworks like dual-process models enhance accuracy and reduce errors, positioning LLMs as effective assistants rather than standalone tools in AES.
Prompting Techniques: Zero-shot and chain-of-thought prompting significantly improve rubric-based evaluation, enabling better handling of subjective criteria in both AES and ASAG.

It begins with an examination of existing systems and their inherent problems, followed by a detailed breakdown of functional and non-functional requirements. State diagrams, use case models, and sequence diagrams are employed to model the proposed Veritas system, ensuring alignment with stakeholder needs and Ethiopian contextual constraints. 3.1. Existing System and Its Problems
3.1.1 Overview of Existing Online Assessment Systems in Ethiopia
In the Ethiopian context, online assessment and monitoring systems are primarily characterized by a mix of foreign-hosted commercial platforms, locally adapted generic tools, and traditional manual processes. These systems are utilized across educational institutions, government agencies, and private enterprises for recruitment, certification, and academic evaluations.
Foreign platforms such as TestGorilla, HackerRank, and ProctorU dominate the market for organizations seeking advanced features. TestGorilla, for instance, offers a suite of pre-built assessments focusing on skills like coding, cognitive abilities, and personality traits, with automated grading for objective questions and basic proctoring via webcam monitoring. HackerRank specializes in technical evaluations, providing coding challenges with real-time compilation and execution, alongside anti-cheating measures like plagiarism detection. ProctorU emphasizes live proctoring, integrating human invigilators with AI-assisted tools for identity verification and behavior analysis, such as eye-tracking and screen recording.
Locally, many institutions rely on open-source or low-cost alternatives like Moodle or Google Forms, customized for basic quiz administration. Moodle, an open-source learning management system (LMS), supports timed quizzes, multiple-choice questions, and rudimentary proctoring through browser lockdowns. In resource-constrained settings, manual processes persist, involving paper-based exams or hybrid setups where subjective responses are emailed and graded by hand. For enterprise recruitment, tools like LinkedIn Assessments or custom spreadsheets are sometimes employed, but these lack integrated monitoring.
These systems operate on a client-server model, with assessments delivered via web browsers or dedicated apps. Data is typically stored on cloud servers hosted abroad (e.g., AWS or Google Cloud), and features like real-time feedback are limited to objective items. Integration with local identity systems, such as Ethiopia's national ID or enterprise credentials, is often absent, leading to reliance on email-based authentication.
3.1.2. Identified Problems and Limitations
Despite their utility, existing systems exhibit several critical shortcomings, particularly when applied to the Ethiopian digital ecosystem. These limitations were identified through stakeholder interviews, observational studies of platforms like ProctorU and Moodle, and a comparative analysis informed by the literature review in Chapter 2. The problems can be categorized into technical, economic, operational, and ethical dimensions.
(a) Technical Limitations
Infrastructure Incompatibility: Many foreign platforms are optimized for high-bandwidth, stable internet connections. In Ethiopia, where internet latency averages 50-100 ms and outages are common (as per Ethio Telecom reports), this results in frequent session timeouts, data loss during submissions, and degraded proctoring quality. For example, ProctorU's live video streaming often fails on 3G/4G networks, leading to incomplete exams.
Lack of Automated Subjective Grading: Current systems excel at objective assessments (e.g., multiple-choice via HackerRank) but rely on manual grading for essays and open-ended questions. This creates bottlenecks, with turnaround times extending to days or weeks, as noted in interviews with HR managers who reported delays impacting recruitment cycles.
Weak Integration with Local Systems: There is no seamless linkage with Ethiopian identity infrastructures, such as the National ID system or enterprise directories. Authentication often defaults to usernames/passwords, increasing vulnerability to identity spoofing.

(b) Economic Limitations
High Subscription Costs: Platforms like TestGorilla and ProctorU require payments in foreign currency (e.g., USD), which is regulated and scarce in Ethiopia. Annual fees can exceed ETB 500,000 for enterprise plans, making them inaccessible to small institutions and startups, as highlighted in the Ministry of Innovation and Technology's 2025 report on Digital Sovereignty.
Dependency on External Hosting: Data storage on foreign servers incurs ongoing costs and risks non-compliance with emerging data protection laws, potentially leading to fines or operational halts.
(c) Operational Limitations
Cheating Vulnerabilities: While some systems include basic proctoring (e.g., tab-switch detection in Safe Exam Browser), they fall short in detecting sophisticated behaviors like proxy testing or collaborative cheating. Observational studies revealed false positives in AI monitoring due to poor lighting or device quality, common in Ethiopian settings.
Scalability Issues: Handling large-scale assessments (e.g., national exams or mass recruitment) strains these systems. Moodle, for instance, struggles with concurrency beyond 500 users without custom scaling, resulting in crashes during peak times.
User Experience and Accessibility Gaps: Interfaces are often not localized (e.g. lacking Amharic support), and restrictive features like browser lockdowns exclude users with disabilities who rely on assistive technologies. As per Chapter 2's gaps, this prioritizes security over inclusivity, leading to higher anxiety and error rates among candidates.
(d) Ethical and Regulatory Limitations
Data Sovereignty Concerns: Storing sensitive assessment data abroad violates the Digital Ethiopia 2030 Strategy's emphasis on keeping Ethiopian data within national borders, exposing it to foreign jurisdiction and potential breaches.
Bias and Privacy Issues: AI components in platforms like Jia and He's IOPS (from literature) introduce biases, such as inaccurate facial recognition for diverse ethnicities, and raise privacy concerns without clear compliance to Ethiopia's Personal Data Protection Proclamation.
Limited Customization: Existing tools do not support multi-tenant environments for multiple organizations, nor do they offer features like automated certificate generation or subscription models tailored to local payment systems (e.g., Telebirr).
These problems underscore the need for a tailored solution like Veritas, which addresses them through local development, AI-enhanced grading, and lightweight proctoring, as modeled in subsequent sections of this chapter.

Enterprise Account creation 
The Enterprise Account Life Cycle diagram illustrates a hybrid-flow process governing an organization’s journey within the Veritas system. It begins with a vertical approval phase where a Pending Approval registration is reviewed by administrators for activation or rejection. Once in a Fully Functional state, the account enters a horizontal operational phase where data is strictly separated. Administrators can toggle the account into a Suspended state to temporarily restrict access without data loss. Finally, the lifecycle concludes with an account closure process that includes a mandatory 90-day retention period for regulatory compliance and recovery, followed by a permanent purge of all organization records.
/

Exam creation flow
The Exam Status Lifecycle diagram illustrates the progression of an assessment through the Veritas system, transitioning from an initial administrative setup phase to a live operational phase and eventual data preservation. The process begins in the Draft state, where questions and randomization rules are configured, before moving to the Scheduled state once the timing and candidate enrollment are finalized. On reaching the predefined start time, the system automatically triggers the Active state, enabling real-time monitoring and proctoring for candidates. Upon conclusion, the exam enters the Closed state to facilitate automated AI grading, finally moving to the Archived status where results are preserved as read-only historical records for auditing purposes.
/
Candidate examination flow 
The Candidate Examination Flow diagram illustrates the student’s journey through a secure assessment session . The process moves from Awaiting Start to an Active Exam state, where the system manages question navigation and background data syncing. To ensure stability on unreliable networks, a Connection Lost state allows for local answer queuing and automatic background synchronization once the connection is restored. The session can be abruptly ended via a Forced Terminated state if a major integrity violation is detected; otherwise, it proceeds to a Reviewing phase before finalization. The lifecycle concludes at the Submitted state, marking the successful and secure completion of the exam.

/














Ai proctoring module state flow
The AI Proctoring & Monitoring diagram illustrates the continuous background surveillance logic used to maintain exam integrity. The process begins in an Active Monitoring state, where the system performs real-time analysis of webcam feeds, mouse movements, and browser activity. When a deviation—such as a tab switch or facial absence—is identified, the system transitions to an Anomaly Detected phase to calculate a risk score and capture evidence. While minor issues allow the system to return to active tracking, persistent or high-severity behaviors move the session into a Flagged state. If the cumulative cheating probability exceeds a set threshold, the system triggers a Critical Violation, alerting administrators and potentially terminating the session to prevent further compromise.



/




Grading module flow 
The Grading & Results Lifecycle diagram illustrates the progression from exam submission to performance reporting. Upon submission, the Pending Grading state uses AI to evaluate responses, routing low-confidence subjective answers to a Manual Review phase for blind human grading. The process then moves to Analytics Calculation, where the system generates rankings and performs a variance analysis to compare human grades against initial AI scores. The lifecycle concludes in the Reporting phase with the delivery of personalized feedback and the optional generation of branded certificates for passing candidates.

Activity Diagrams
Activity diagrams illustrate the dynamic behavior of the system by modeling workflows, decision points, and parallel activities involved in the AI-Enhanced Online Assessment and Monitoring System. These diagrams provide a clear, step-by-step representation of how different users interact with the system and how the system responds to various conditions. Activity diagrams are particularly effective in representing complex processes such as online examinations, AI-based proctoring, automated grading, and administrative decision-making, where conditional logic and exception handling are essential.
The system’s activity diagrams are organized into four main workflows:
Enterprise Registration & Authentication
This activity diagram illustrates the process through which an enterprise registers and gains access to the system. The workflow begins when an organization submits its registration details. The system then forwards the request for administrative approval. A decision node determines whether the registration is approved or rejected. If approved, the enterprise selects a subscription plan and completes payment before accessing the system dashboard. If the payment fails or the registration is rejected, the system notifies the enterprise accordingly and terminates the process. This workflow ensures controlled system access and subscription enforcement.
/
Exam Creation & Scheduling
This activity diagram represents the steps followed by an administrator to create and schedule an examination. The process starts with the administrator logging into the system and opening the exam creation module. The administrator configures exam parameters such as question types, duration, passing score, and randomization rules. A decision point determines whether candidates are added immediately or the exam is saved as a draft. Once candidates are assigned and the schedule is confirmed, the exam is published. This workflow supports flexibility while maintaining structured exam configuration.
/
Candidate Exam + AI Proctoring
This activity diagram describes the flow of events when a candidate takes an examination. The process begins when the candidate logs in using a secure token or credentials. The system performs identity verification using facial recognition before allowing exam access. During the exam, AI-based proctoring continuously monitors candidate behavior. A decision node evaluates whether suspicious behavior is detected. If a violation occurs, the system logs the event and may terminate the exam depending on severity. Otherwise, the candidate continues until submission or time expiration. This workflow emphasizes exam integrity and real-time monitoring.

/
Grading + Certificate Generation
This activity diagram illustrates the post-exam evaluation process. It begins when an exam submission is received by the system. Objective questions are graded automatically, while subjective answers, if present, are forwarded for manual blind grading. The system then calculates the final score and analyzes proctoring logs to determine cheating probability. A decision point checks whether the candidate meets both academic and integrity requirements. Successful candidates receive a digital certificate, while unsuccessful or suspicious cases are flagged for review. This workflow ensures fair assessment and result transparency.
